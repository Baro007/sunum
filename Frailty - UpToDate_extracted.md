Frailty
INTRODUCTION
Frailty is a clinical syndrome of aging-related physiological vulnerability characterized by reduced resilience to stressors and increased risk of
adverse health outcomes. It reflects cumulative declines across multiple systems, resulting in impaired homeostatic reserve and recovery
capacity. Clinically, frailty often presents as decreased energy, muscle weakness, slowed performance, and unintentional weight loss, even in
the absence of overt disease. Recognition of frailty is essential to guide care planning, calibrate interventions, and avoid iatrogenic harm.
This topic will review the epidemiology, conceptual frameworks, biologic mechanisms, diagnostic approaches, and clinical management
strategies relevant to frailty in older adults.
Failure to thrive is discussed separately. (See "Failure to thrive in older adults: Evaluation" and "Failure to thrive in older adults:
Management".)
DEFINITION
Frailty is widely recognized as a clinical syndrome reflecting age-related vulnerability and diminished physiological reserve, resulting in an
increased risk of adverse health outcomes when exposed to stressors such as illness, injury, or surgery [1,2]. Frailty is distinct from but often
overlaps with disability and comorbidity; it is not an inevitable consequence of aging, nor does old age alone define frailty [3-7].
Frailty exists along a continuum, from robust to pre-frail to frail, with the potential for reversibility if identified early. In its most advanced
form, often described as "failure to thrive," even minor stressors can lead to significant decline. (See "Failure to thrive in older adults:
Evaluation" and "Failure to thrive in older adults: Management".)

Key components — Key components of frailty include unintentional weight loss, fatigue or reduced energy, decreased muscle mass and
strength, slowed gait or physical performance, and some degree of vulnerability in function.
EPIDEMIOLOGY
Prevalence — The prevalence of frailty varies widely depending on the population studied, the specific frailty assessment tool used, and
regional or socioeconomic factors [1,14-16]. Among community-dwelling older adults, prevalence estimates using physical frailty measures
(eg, Fried phenotype) typically range from 11 to 15 percent, while deficit accumulation models (eg, Frailty Index) yield estimates of 20 to 29
percent. Pre-frailty is more common, affecting 45 to 49 percent of community-dwelling older adults [17,18]. In middle-aged adults (45 to 64
years), frailty is less common, with prevalence estimates ranging from 5 to 11 percent [19-21].
A 2021 global report covering 62 countries showed that frailty is more prevalent in low-resource countries compared with high-resource
countries, and it is more common among women, those with lower education, and those living alone or in poverty [16]. Studies in Asian and

PATHOPHYSIOLOGY
Frailty arises from the dysregulation of multiple interrelated physiologic systems, involving chronic inflammation, sarcopenia, hormonal
imbalance, mitochondrial dysfunction, and impaired metabolic signaling. These biologic alterations, often accelerated by aging, disease, and
environmental stressors, undermine physiologic reserve and increase vulnerability to minor stress ( figure 1) [4]. Emerging contributors
include extracellular vesicle signaling [79-83], alterations to the gut microbiome [84-91], biologic clocks [92-95], and circulating cell-free
deoxyribonucleic acid (cfDNA) [96-101].
Key mechanistic pathways include:
Postoperative complications – Frail patients undergoing surgery have a higher risk of 30-day postoperative mortality, infections,
delirium, and extended hospital stays compared with non-frail patients [35-37]. For example, in a meta-analysis, frail patients undergoing
emergency surgery had nearly a fourfold increased odds of 30-day mortality compared with non-frail patients (odds ratio [OR] 3.85, 95% CI
2.83-5.24) [38].
●
Falls and fractures – In meta-analyses, frailty has been associated with an increased risk of falls and fractures [39-42]. Further, older frail
patients who experience falls are at higher risk of prolonged hospital stays and complications [43].
●
Hospitalization and institutionalization – Frailty is associated with an increased likelihood of unplanned hospital admissions and long-
term care placements among older adults [44].
●
Mental health outcomes – Frailty appears to influence mental health outcomes [45-47], including an increased risk of suicidal ideation
and suicide attempts [48-51].
●
Progression and impact of chronic disease – Individuals with both frailty and chronic diseases exhibit a bidirectional relationship, where
frailty increases the risk of developing chronic diseases, while chronic diseases can exacerbate frailty [52]. For example, older adults with
both frailty and diabetes face the highest mortality risk, compared with those with either condition alone [53]. Frailty is implicated in the
accelerated progression of neurodegenerative diseases, including Alzheimer disease and vascular dementia [54,55].
●
Mortality – Frailty increases the risk of all-cause mortality [56]. Frailty is associated with higher in-hospital mortality in older adults with
acute myocardial infarction [57-62]. Across diverse populations and frailty assessment tools, adjusted hazard ratios for all-cause mortality
in frail versus non-frail older adults with cancer typically range from approximately 2.1 to 2.8, even after controlling for age, cancer stage,
sex, comorbidities, and cancer type [63-71]. During the coronavirus disease 2019 (COVID-19) pandemic, frailty predicted mortality more
strongly than age or comorbidity alone [72-77]. In the COVID-OLD study, patients with higher Clinical Frailty Scale scores faced up to
threefold greater risk of death during hospitalization compared with less frail peers [78].
●
Chronic inflammation – Frailty is associated with chronic, low-grade inflammation, termed inflammaging [92,102-106], marked by
elevated levels of interleukin 6 (IL-6), tumor necrosis factor alpha (TNF-alpha), C-reactive protein (CRP), and soluble TNF receptors. These
cytokines result in impaired adaptive immunity, reduced vaccine efficacy, and greater susceptibility to infection [107-111], as well as
hematologic effects, including anemia of inflammation [112-117], endothelial dysfunction, and thrombosis, due to increased levels of
fibrinogen, D-dimer, and Factor VIII [111,118,119].
●
Sarcopenia and musculoskeletal decline – Age-related decline in muscle mass and strength is a hallmark of frailty, driven by
inflammation, hormonal changes, and mitochondrial dysfunction, as well as reduced satellite cell function and regenerative capacity with
age [120-122]. Sarcopenia and frailty frequently coexist and reinforce each other [123-126].
●
Endocrine dysregulation – Multiple age-related hormone changes have been associated with frailty. Age-related shifts in hormonal
balance contribute to frailty by promoting catabolism, reducing musculoskeletal integrity, and impairing stress responses [122]. Key
alterations include decreased levels of growth hormone, insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate, testosterone,
estrogen, and vitamin D; increased cortisol; and alterations in the renin angiotensin system. Clinical correlates include decreased strength,
balance, mobility, muscle mass, and immune function, as well as greater bone loss and fall risk [127-150].

MEASUREMENT TOOLS
A wide array of frailty measurement tools have been validated, capturing different domains and offering unique advantages and limitations
[1,163-167]. Most tools reliably identify individuals at highest risk for adverse outcomes, but their agreement and predictive performance can
vary depending on the population and context [165,168-171].
Choosing a frailty assessment tool — When choosing a frailty assessment tool, clinicians should consider the care setting, time constraints,
and intended use [163].
Fried Frailty Phenotype — One of the most widely used physical frailty tools is the Fried Frailty Phenotype, which defines frailty based on
five criteria [3]. Individuals meeting three or more criteria are classified as frail, one or two as pre-frail, and none as robust ( table 1):
This tool, validated in the Cardiovascular Health Study (CHS), which involved over 5000 adults aged ≥ 65 years [3], is predictive of adverse
clinical outcomes but requires direct physical measurements, which may limit its feasibility in some settings.
Frailty Index — The FI quantifies frailty as a ratio of the number of deficits present to the total number assessed [172]. It requires answering
20 or more medical and function-related questions. The higher the number of deficits, the higher the frailty score. This multidimensional tool
can be adapted to information available in medical records and does not require patient examination. The FIꞌs predictive validity for mortality
and hospitalization is well established [10,173-180].
Rapid screening tools — Although the Fried Frailty Phenotype assessment and the FI approach are the most commonly cited and most
validated approaches to frailty measurement, clinicians and patients may benefit from using a quicker frailty screening assessment tool.
Studies comparing the most commonly utilized screening tools indicate that rapid screening tools are comparable to other tools in
predicting risk of adverse health outcomes and mortality [181-183].
Some examples include:
Mitochondrial dysfunction – Mitochondrial dysfunction is increasingly recognized as a central contributor to frailty in older adults,
resulting in faster depletion of muscle energy stores during exertion, contributing to decreased physical performance and increased
fatigability [151-156]. Measurement of mitochondrial respiration may serve as a minimally invasive biomarker for frailty [154,157].
●
Altered nutrient sensing and metabolism – Disruption in nutrient sensing and metabolic regulation is increasingly recognized as a core
mechanism contributing to frailty [158,159]. Pathways such as mechanistic target of rapamycin (mTOR), adenosine monophosphate-
activated protein kinase (AMPK) [1,160], and the insulin/IGF-1 [161] axis are crucial regulators of cellular energy balance, anabolic
signaling, and stress responses. These pathways modulate the body's ability to adapt to nutritional and energetic stressors, and their
impairment can promote catabolism, leading to frailty [162].
●
Routine clinical settings – There are several brief, validated screening tools suitable for primary care and community settings, with high
feasibility and diagnostic accuracy (see 'Rapid screening tools' below). More detailed instruments, such as the Fried Frailty Phenotype and
the Frailty Index (FI), are valuable in comprehensive evaluations. (See 'Fried Frailty Phenotype' below and 'Frailty Index' below.)
●
Prior to treatments or interventions – When frailty assessment is used to inform high-stakes treatment decisions (eg, chemotherapy or
surgery), tools validated in those specific populations may offer more accurate predictions than general-purpose tools [1]. eFrailty.org
provides a helpful guide for selecting appropriate frailty instruments, along with electronic calculators for commonly used tools.

DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Early identification of frailty in clinical practice is essential for guiding further assessment, tailoring interventions, and optimizing outcomes
for older adults. The diagnosis is made based on a combination of history, physical examination findings, and the use of a frailty scale. When
applying most frailty instruments, clinicians are advised to rate a person’s usual health state over the preceding weeks to months, rather
than a transient decline during an acute illness or hospitalization.

Physical examination — The physical examination should be comprehensive and include evaluation of any specific symptoms, including
those of unintentional weight loss or fatigue. (See "Approach to the patient with unintentional weight loss", section on 'Physical examination'
and "Approach to the adult patient with fatigue", section on 'Physical examination'.)
If a frailty screening tool performed during the initial history suggests frailty, a full frailty assessment should be conducted using a validated
instrument.

Conditions that contribute to frailty
DIFFERENTIAL DIAGNOSIS
Frailty is a clinical syndrome, not a diagnosis of exclusion. However, it is critical to consider reversible or treatable conditions that can mimic
or contribute to frailty. These include:
Cognition and mood – Changes in memory, attention, or executive function and depressive symptoms may exacerbate frailty. (See
"Mental status scales to evaluate cognition" and "Screening for depression in adults", section on 'Screening tests'.)
●
Sleep disturbances – Sleep disturbances are common in older adults and may contribute to fatigue, cognitive decline, and worsening
frailty [199,200]. As part of the frailty assessment, clinicians should routinely ask about sleep quality, duration, nighttime awakenings,
excessive daytime sleepiness, and use of sleep aids. Validated tools such as the Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (PSQI) are recommended to
screen for sleep quality, duration, insomnia symptoms, and daytime dysfunction, as poor sleep quality and abnormal sleep duration (short
<5 hours or long >8 to 9 hours) are independently associated with frailty and pre-frailty.
●
Social support and recent changes – Social isolation, recent hospitalizations, or changes in living situation can affect frailty and may
affect the treatment plan.
●
Occult malignancy – Unintentional weight loss, anemia, and constitutional symptoms should prompt evaluation for underlying cancer
(eg, lymphoma, myeloma, solid tumor). Patients should have all age-appropriate cancer screening. (See "Overview of preventive care in
adults", section on 'Cancer screening'.)
●
Depression – Depression can present with fatigue, weight loss, cognitive complaints, social withdrawal, and functional decline, all of which
can mimic or exacerbate frailty. Symptoms such as persistent low mood, feelings of worthlessness, hopelessness, guilt, or anhedonia may
help distinguish it from frailty alone. (See "Approach to the adult patient with suspected depression".)
●
Heart failure – Heart failure can also present with fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, weight changes, and functional decline. Symptoms
such as dyspnea, orthopnea, and peripheral edema may help distinguish it from frailty alone. (See "Approach to diagnosis and evaluation
of acute decompensated heart failure in adults".)
●
Chronic kidney disease – The condition may cause fatigue, poor appetite, weight loss, cognitive changes, and functional decline,
especially in advanced stages. Laboratory evaluation of kidney function is essential to the diagnosis. (See "Chronic kidney disease (newly
identified): Clinical presentation and diagnostic approach in adults".)
●
Hypothyroidism – Hypothyroidism can present with fatigue, weight gain or loss, cognitive slowing, and functional impairment, often with
subtle or nonspecific symptoms in older adults. Laboratory evaluation is essential to the diagnosis. (See "Diagnosis of and screening for
hypothyroidism in nonpregnant adults".)
●
Anemia – Anemia can cause fatigue, weakness, and exercise intolerance. Etiologies include iron, B12, or folate deficiency, chronic disease,
or myelodysplasia. (See "Diagnostic approach to anemia in adults".)
●
Malnutrition/vitamin deficiencies – Protein-energy malnutrition and deficiencies in vitamins (A, D, E, B12, C) are common in older adults
and can cause or worsen frailty, cognitive decline, and functional impairment [201]. (See "Nutritional issues in older adults".)
●
Parkinson disease – Parkinson disease may present with bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor, gait disturbance, and nonmotor symptoms such as
sleep dysfunction, depression, and cognitive decline. (See "Diagnosis and differential diagnosis of Parkinson disease".)
●
Other neurodegenerative disorders – Disorders such as dementia with Lewy bodies, frontotemporal dementia, and amyotrophic lateral
sclerosis can contribute to frailty through motor, cognitive, and behavioral symptoms. (See "Clinical features and diagnosis of dementia
with Lewy bodies" and "Frontotemporal dementia: Clinical features and diagnosis" and "Clinical features of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis
and other forms of motor neuron disease".)
●
Systemic vasculitis/polymyalgia rheumatica – These conditions can present with fatigue, weight loss, proximal muscle pain/stiffness,
and elevated inflammatory markers. Testing of erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), C-reactive protein (CRP), and autoantibodies may be
helpful. (See "Overview of and approach to the vasculitides in adults", section on 'Diagnostic approach' and "Clinical manifestations and
diagnosis of polymyalgia rheumatica", section on 'Diagnostic approach'.)
●
Acute cerebrovascular event (stroke/transient ischemic attack) – Sudden onset of focal neurologic deficits, cognitive changes, or
functional decline should prompt urgent neuroimaging and evaluation. (See "Initial evaluation and management of transient ischemic
attack and minor ischemic stroke".)

MANAGEMENT
Multicomponent approach — Management of frailty should be individualized and multidisciplinary, addressing physical, nutritional,
psychologic, and social domains. Effective care includes both structured interventions and practical, common-sense approaches that can be
implemented across settings, with an awareness of goals of care ( figure 3).
Multicomponent interventions that combine exercise, nutrition, cognitive training, and social support appear to yield the best outcomes for
frailty prevention and management [202-204]. In particular, meta-analyses have shown that combining exercise with nutritional support
yields benefits in reducing frailty among older adults [205,206]. Multicomponent interventions that also incorporate cognitive training,
psychosocial support, and medication optimization have demonstrated broader efficacy in mitigating frailty-related outcomes [207].
General measures in management — The following measures are appropriate for all frail and pre-frail adults [202,208-211].
Comprehensive geriatric assessment — A comprehensive geriatric assessment (CGA) is essential to providing a complete assessment of
function and prognosis and can guide the development of management and intervention plans. (See "Comprehensive geriatric
assessment".)
Goals of care discussion — A goals of care discussion is important for all older adults, particularly those with frailty (see "Discussing goals of
care"). For example, screening or intervention for some conditions may not provide an appreciable benefit, and some procedures or
hospitalizations may pose an unnecessary burden [215]. In some cases, referral to palliative care may be the most appropriate intervention.
(See 'Palliative care' below.)
However, many frail older adults may still benefit from some interventions, including those targeting specific components of their frailty
examination. Input from a multidisciplinary team evaluating medical, functional, cognitive, psychologic, and social issues, followed by a
coordinated care plan, is often the best way to prioritize care for frail older adults with multimorbidity [202,216].
Palliative care — Diagnosing frailty helps identify patients at highest risk of adverse health outcomes. Palliative care approaches may be
helpful to alleviate symptoms and to help determine the appropriateness of potential medical and surgical interventions. For those with
advanced frailty and multiple comorbidities or those with failure to thrive, the engagement of a palliative care team may help to identify
methods to preserve or improve quality of life and to clarify goals of care. (See "Benefits, services, and models of subspecialty palliative
care", section on 'Palliative care services'.)
Exercise — Structured, multicomponent exercise programs (eg, including strength, balance, aerobic, flexibility) are appropriate interventions
for both frailty prevention and slowing of frailty progression, similar to older adult populations in general (see "Physical activity and exercise
in older adults", section on 'Overview of physical activity components'). Group-based and supervised programs show benefit in improved
mobility, muscle strength, and reduced falls [203,204,217]. However, long-term outcomes are uncertain.
In a 2024 meta-analysis of 28 trials involving 4857 older adults, structured multicomponent exercise programs of at least 12 weeks' duration
led to measurable improvements in both frailty status and physical performance [218]. However, the benefits were modest, and in most
trials, either participants or study personnel were not blinded.

Nutrition — Systematic reviews have shown inconsistent effects of nutritional strategies in improving frailty status or physical performance
[223-225]. For patients with weight loss, attention should be focused on causes of malnutrition, which may include medication side effects,
depression, difficulties with chewing and swallowing, dependency on others for eating, and adherence to unnecessary dietary restrictions
(low-salt/low-fat) ( table 3).
Although oral nutritional supplements between meals (low-volume, high-calorie drinks or puddings) may help add protein and calories, their
overall benefit is limited. (See "Failure to thrive in older adults: Management", section on 'Weight loss'.)
Vitamin D supplementation in those with low levels may reduce falls, improve balance, and preserve muscle strength. This is discussed
separately. (See "Falls: Prevention in community-dwelling older persons" and "Geriatric health maintenance", section on 'Vitamin D'.)
Limited role for pharmacologic interventions — No pharmacologic agent is established as a first-line treatment for frailty, and most
interventions have shown only modest benefits in clinically meaningful outcomes [226-228]. Ineffective therapies include estrogen therapy,
growth hormone therapy, and dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate (DHEA-S) supplementation [229-231]. Testosterone replacement may improve
muscle mass or strength in males with hypogonadism, but routine use is not recommended due to limited efficacy and potential adverse
effects. This is discussed separately. (See "Testosterone treatment of male hypogonadism", section on 'Muscle strength/fat-free mass'.)
Some studies suggest that angiotensin-converting enzyme inhibitors and angiotensin receptor blockers may improve muscle strength and
physical function, but results are inconsistent and insufficient to support routine use [232-235]. Emerging studies suggest that metformin
may reduce inflammation, cellular senescence, and muscle fibrosis, with early trials in older adults showing improved muscle recovery after
disuse. Lactoferrin and other anti-inflammatory or immunomodulatory compounds are under investigation, but robust clinical data are
lacking [236-238].
MODELS OF CARE
An interdisciplinary team-based approach to care is often best at meeting the needs of frail adults. However, outcomes of interdisciplinary
interventions are mixed. The frailest older adults in the United States may benefit from the Medicare Program of All-inclusive Care for the
Elderly (PACE). PACE was designated as a permanent Medicare program in 1997 because it was successful in maintaining function and
community-dwelling status and in providing lower-cost care compared with traditional fee-for-service care [239]. The goals of this model are
to improve function, overcome environmental challenges, and keep older adults living in their communities by preventing
institutionalization.
In this outpatient patient management model, primary care is delivered to community-dwelling older adults by an interdisciplinary team in a
day clinic setting [240]. The interdisciplinary team includes a practitioner knowledgeable in geriatric medicine, nurses, physical and
occupational therapists, and social workers. Services provided include home nursing, physical and occupational therapy, transportation, aide
service, and adult day care. Patients who enter the program receive complete long-term care and are followed until the end of their lives,
even if they eventually require placement in an assisted-living facility or nursing home.
SUMMARY AND RECOMMENDATIONS
Definition – Frailty is a multidimensional clinical syndrome of diminished physiologic reserve and impaired stress response, resulting in
increased risk of adverse health outcomes, including falls, hospitalization, cognitive decline, and mortality. While more common with age,
frailty is distinct from aging alone, and its presence varies widely among older adults. (See 'Definition' above.)
●
Epidemiology and health consequences – The prevalence of frailty increased with age and is more common in patients with comorbid
conditions. Health consequences are diverse and include a greater risk of postoperative complications, fractures, institutionalization, and
mortality. (See 'Epidemiology' above.)
●
Pathophysiology – Frailty arises from the interplay of chronic inflammation, hormonal dysregulation, mitochondrial dysfunction,
sarcopenia, and altered nutrient sensing. These mechanisms collectively impair resilience and contribute to progressive decline. (See
'Pathophysiology' above.)

Table 2: Sleep hygiene guidelines
Recommendation Details
Regular bedtime and rise time Having a consistent bedtime and rise time leads to more regular sleep schedules and avoids periods of sleepdeprivation or periods of extended wakefulness during the night.
Avoid napping Avoid napping, especially naps lasting longer than 1 hour and naps late in the day.
Limit caffeine Avoid caffeine after lunch. The time between lunch and bedtime represents approximately 2 half-lives forcaffeine, and this time window allows for most caffeine to be metabolized before bedtime.
Limit alcohol Recommendations are typically focused on avoiding alcohol near bedtime. Alcohol is initially sedating, butactivating as it is metabolized. Alcohol also negatively impacts sleep architecture.
Avoid nicotine Nicotine is a stimulant and should be avoided near bedtime and at night.
Exercise Daytime physical activity is encouraged, in particular, 4 to 6 hours before bedtime, as this may facilitate sleeponset. Rigorous exercise within 2 hours of bedtime is discouraged.
Keep the sleep environment quiet and dark Noise and light exposure during the night can disrupt sleep. White noise or ear plugs are often recommendedto reduce disturbing noise. Using blackout shades or an eye mask is commonly recommended to reduce light.
This may also include avoiding exposure to television or technology near bedtime, as this can have an impacton circadian rhythms by shifting sleep timing later.
Bedroom clock Avoid checking the time at night. This includes alarm clocks and other time pieces (eg, watches and smartphones). Checking the time increases cognitive arousal and prolongs wakefulness.
Evening eating Avoid a large meal close to bedtime. Eat a healthy and filling (but not too heavy) meal in the early evening andavoid late-night snacks.
